### Key Drivers of Gold (GC=F) Fundamentals  
While traditional financial statements are absent, the following factors critically influence GC=F pricing:  

1. **Real Interest Rates & Opportunity Cost**:  
   Gold prices exhibit an inverse correlation with real yields (nominal yields minus inflation expectations). Rising real rates increase the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding gold, pressuring prices downward—and vice versa.  

2. **US Dollar Strength**:  
   As gold is priced in USD, a stronger dollar makes gold more expensive for foreign buyers, reducing demand. The DXY (US Dollar Index) is a key leading indicator.  

3. **Geopolitical & Systemic Risk**:  
   Gold acts as a safe-haven asset during periods of heightened risk (e.g., conflicts, banking crises, political instability), often decoupling from traditional correlations.  

4. **Central Bank Policies & Reserves**:  
   Purchases/sales by central banks (especially emerging markets like China, India, and Russia) directly impact physical demand. Forward guidance on monetary policy shapes investor expectations.  

5. **Inflation Expectations**:  
   Gold is widely viewed as an inflation hedge. Surprises in CPI/PCE data or shifts in long-term inflation breakevens can trigger rapid repricing.  

6. **Supply Dynamics**:  
   Mine production, recycling rates, and hedging activity by producers affect physical supply. However, for futures like GC=F, paper market flows (speculative positioning, ETF flows) often dominate short-term price action.  

7. **Technical & Positioning Factors**:  
   Commitments of Traders (COT) data, open interest, and moving average crossovers (e.g., 50-day/200-day) provide insights into market sentiment and potential turning points.
